1. Executive Summary

This report is a sample structure for evaluating the official domain of a local government using publicly available information (OSINT: Open Source Intelligence) and an attacker’s perspective on the exposed attack surface. Based on the concept of Active Defense, it summarizes in a technically accurate and accountability-oriented way “what is visible from the outside” and “what kinds of risks can be expected around web and email.”

1.1 High-level Assessment

ItemAssessmentSummary
Attack Surface Exposure Medium The number of exposed subdomains is limited, but email-related configuration and historical traces of infrastructure create room for an attacker to gain initial footholds.
Email Security (SPF / DMARC) Needs Improvement An SPF record is configured, but the DMARC policy is set to p=none, which means current protection against spoofed emails is not sufficient.
Web / Application Generally Good The web server sends X-Frame-Options, X-XSS-Protection, and X-Content-Type-Options headers. From a basic security header standpoint, the configuration can be considered standard and reasonably controlled.
Threat Reputation / OSINT Good Across multiple reputation sources, the domain is not currently flagged as malicious and holds a generally positive reputation.

1.2 Key Risks and Recommendations (Summary)

  • Short Gradually strengthen the DMARC policy from p=none to quarantine and ultimately to reject.
  • Short Review the email-related hostnames and rationalize unused subdomains by decommissioning or redirecting them under a clear policy.
  • Mid Conduct OSINT-based attack surface reviews once or twice a year to clean up stale data such as old DNS records and outdated certificates, and to reduce unnecessary exposure.
  • Mid Introduce mock phishing exercises to measure staff email open and click-through rates, and use the results to strengthen security training and internal rules.
  • Long Prepare for future 24/7 SOC / MDR engagement by gradually building a logging foundation and an incident first-response playbook.

In simpler terms: the recommended path is (1) improve email anti-spoofing, (2) perform regular clean-up of exposed information, and (3) build a bridge toward a full monitoring capability in the future.

2. Scope & Methodology

2.1 Scope

  • Target domain: Redacted (official local government domain).
  • Related subdomains: Multiple subdomains identified through OSINT-based enumeration.
  • In scope: Attack surface (DNS / web / email), OSINT-based threat reputation, email security settings (SPF / DMARC).
  • Out of scope: Internal network scanning, log review, full-scale vulnerability scanning, and intrusive testing. This report is strictly limited to an assessment based on publicly available information.

2.2 Platforms and Techniques Used

Attack Surface / OSINT OSINT

  • Cross-referenced multiple OSINT platforms that aggregate public internet data.
  • Reviewed DNS records, certificate transparency logs, and Passive DNS datasets.
  • Used in-house scripts to enumerate and consolidate subdomains.

Mail, DNS, and Web Evaluation Mail & Web

  • Verified DNS records using standard DNS client tools.
  • Checked SPF / DMARC / blacklist status via email security diagnostic platforms.
  • Analyzed HTTP response headers with common web inspection tools.
Collection and analysis of public information were performed entirely using passive methods that do not impose load on the target systems, and in accordance with applicable laws and guidelines.

  • However, certificate logs and DNS history indicate that hostnames historically in use may remain in records. These remnants can persist through infrastructure changes and should be considered in future clean-up efforts.

4. DNS / Email Security (SPF / DMARC)

4.1 SPF Record

SPF: "v=spf1 ip4:***.***.***.0/26 ip4:***.***.***.236 ip4:***.***.***.217 ip4:***.***.***.19 a:[mail platform hostname] include:[external mail service] -all"

Findings

  • The SPF record is present and syntactically valid.
  • Multiple IP addresses and external services are authorized, which suggests that some historical elements may still be included.
  • In the longer term, it is advisable to narrow the authorized senders to those actually in use and remove obsolete entries, simplifying the SPF configuration and reducing confusion.

4.2 DMARC Record

DMARC: "v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc-report@example.jp"

Findings

  • A DMARC record is published, but the policy p=none means it is effectively used for monitoring only.
  • In this state, receiving mail servers are not instructed to quarantine or reject messages based on DMARC results, and therefore the protection against spoofed emails remains limited.
  • We recommend planning a phased transition toward quarantine and eventually reject, supported by DMARC reporting analysis and test deployments.

4.3 MX / Blacklist Check

  • Major spam blacklists do not currently list the domain or its related IP addresses as malicious.
  • Email operations for local governments often involve prefectural or shared centers. In the event of an outage or blacklist listing, it is important to have clear responsibility boundaries among the local government, shared centers, and external providers.

5. Threat Reputation

We reviewed threat intelligence and reputation sources as well as Passive DNS data for the target domain. The key observations are as follows.

5.1 Detection Summary

  • Across multiple security vendor datasets, the domain is not flagged as malicious at this time.
  • The community-driven score is positive, with no notable malicious reports.
  • Automated categorization: Government / Legal.
At the time of review, there is no indication that the domain has been used for malware distribution or phishing. The overall reputation can be considered good.

5.2 Passive DNS and Historical View

  • Over the past several years, the domain has been associated with multiple IP addresses, all within government networks or domestic hosting providers.
  • There is no strong indication that the IPs in use are heavily shared with a large number of malicious domains; the shared infrastructure appears relatively clean.

6.1 Improvement Suggestions Based on Drill Results

  • Run mock phishing drills once or twice a year and track trends over time, rather than treating it as a one-off exercise.
  • For management and senior staff, emphasize “model behavior” and proper reporting procedures.
  • Consider configuring email clients to always show the full sender address and full domain, so suspicious senders are easier to recognize.

7. Risk Assessment

RiskLevelDescription
Spoofed emails misleading residents or staff High With DMARC set to p=none, there is a residual risk that spoofed emails are delivered as if they were legitimate messages from the organization.
Attack surface growth due to legacy configurations Medium Unused subdomains or outdated SPF entries can provide additional information and opportunities for attackers.
Lack of proactive OSINT-based situational awareness Medium Attackers typically use OSINT to select and profile targets. If defenders do not maintain a comparable level of visibility, incident detection and response can lag behind.
Unclear first response to security incidents Medium Without a clearly defined first-response playbook and contact structure, decision-making and reporting during the first 24 hours of an incident may be delayed.

8. Recommendations

8.1 Short Term (0–6 Months)

  • Start the evaluation and testing needed to gradually move the DMARC policy from none to quarantine and ultimately to reject.
  • Inventory unused subdomains (such as legacy email hostnames), and decide whether to decommission or redirect them under a clear policy.
  • Run a proof-of-concept mock phishing exercise and use the outcome to design and prioritize staff security awareness training.
  • Put simply, this phase is about “reducing email spoofing risk” by tightening sender configuration and strengthening staff awareness.

8.2 Mid Term (6–18 Months)

  • Embed attack surface / OSINT checks into the annual security cycle and treat them as regular “exposure clean-ups.”
  • Develop an incident first-response guideline that clearly defines internal roles, reporting lines, and how to coordinate with external providers and shared centers.
  • Define a log retention policy (what logs are needed, where they are stored, and how long they are kept).
  • In simpler terms, this phase is about “writing down who does what when something happens,” and ensuring the necessary logs are available afterwards.

8.3 Long Term (18+ Months)

  • Build a roadmap that assumes future collaboration with 24/7 SOC / MDR providers, taking into account the split of responsibilities between local governments, prefectural bodies, shared centers, and vendors.
  • Consider a gradual move toward Zero Trust and identity-centric security as cloud adoption (SaaS / IaaS) increases.
  • In simpler terms, this phase is about “drawing a long-term map” so that when the organization is ready to work with a full monitoring service, the transition is smooth.

9. Incident First 24 Hours Guide (Summary)

The following summarizes the minimum actions recommended during the first 24 hours of a major security incident (such as data leakage or external attacks). A more detailed playbook can be prepared as a separate document.

  1. Detection and initial report (0–1 hour)
    Immediately notify the designated internal contact point, classify the suspected incident type, and make an initial report to senior management.
  2. Preliminary impact assessment (1–4 hours)
    Identify affected systems, accounts, and data categories; secure relevant logs; and send early notifications to shared centers and key external providers.
  3. Technical containment (4–12 hours)
    Implement measures such as account lockouts, network segmentation, or temporary suspension of affected services to prevent further damage.
  4. External communication planning (12–24 hours)
    Draft communication for residents and media, prepare FAQs, and set up or confirm a contact point for incoming enquiries.
